When financial institutions, insurance companies, rental car agencies and other corporations deceive or take advantage of consumers through deceptive and unfair practices, we level the playing field.

Consumer protection law exists to ensure that corporations cannot take advantage of individuals through fraud, deception, or unfair business practices. When companies violate these protections, class action lawsuits provide an effective remedy for affected consumers.

Legal Framework for Consumer Protection

Multiple federal and state laws provide robust protections for consumers against unfair and deceptive trade practices.

  • Florida Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA) - Chapter 501, Florida Statutes (501.204)
  • Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act - 15 U.S.C. § 2301 et seq.
  • Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) - 15 U.S.C. § 1681 et seq.
  • Truth in Lending Act (TILA) - 15 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq.
  • Fair Debt Collection Practices Act - 15 U.S.C. § 1692 et seq.

These statutes provide significant remedies including actual damages, attorney's fees, and injunctive relief for affected consumers.

Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Trade Practices

Consumer protection violations can take many forms, from outright fraud to subtle deceptive practices. Common violations include:

Common Consumer Protection Violations

Deceptive Advertising

  • False advertising - Misleading claims about products, services, or pricing
  • Bait and switch tactics - Advertising products at low prices then pushing higher-priced alternatives
  • Hidden fees and charges - Undisclosed costs added to transactions
  • Comparison deception - False comparisons with competitors

Unfair Business Practices

  • Warranty violations - Refusing to honor legitimate warranty claims
  • Defective products - Selling products known to be defective or dangerous
  • Auto dealer fraud - Misrepresentation of vehicle condition, financing terms
  • Service contract deception - Misleading extended warranty offerings

Financial Services Violations

  • Unfair debt collection - Aggressive or deceptive collection practices
  • Credit reporting errors - Failure to investigate disputed information
  • Predatory lending - Deceptive mortgage and loan practices

Product/Service Issues

  • Non-delivery of goods - Taking payment without providing services
  • Quality misrepresentation - Products not meeting advertised standards
  • Return policy violations - Refusing legitimate returns or refunds

"Trade or commerce" includes the advertising, soliciting, providing, offering, or distributing of any good or service, or any property, whether tangible or intangible, real, personal, or mixed, and any other article, commodity, or thing of value, wherever situated.

Who Can You Complain to in Florida?

Florida consumers have multiple avenues for reporting unfair and deceptive trade practices:

Florida Attorney General's Office: The primary enforcement agency for consumer protection violations in Florida.

Federal Trade Commission (FTC): Federal agency that handles interstate commerce and national consumer protection issues.

Better Business Bureau: While not a government agency, provides dispute resolution services and maintains business ratings.

How to Pursue a Consumer Protection Claim

Florida consumers have multiple avenues for pursuing consumer protection claims and reporting unfair practices.

Possible Penalties for Deceptive Practices

  • Civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation for businesses
  • Attorney's fees and costs for successful consumer plaintiffs
  • Actual damages plus additional statutory damages as provided by law
